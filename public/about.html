A sandbox for learning to compose

UGPlayground is an interactive playground where young people build their own words for what their character can do — and then watch those words run.

Why this exists

Most tools for teaching computational thinking ask the learner to follow a script: drag this block into that slot, press play, see the expected result. UGPlayground turns that around. There is no expected result. There is a small alphabet of moves and shapes, an editor for each, and a single line where you type combinations and watch what happens.

The site is built around one premise: you can't predict what someone will build once they have their own words. Naming a move, naming a shape, chaining them together — these are tiny acts of language design, and they carry the same generative power language always has. A beginner who has saved five moves and three shapes already has a combinatorial space too big to enumerate. The reward isn't completing a task; it's discovering what their own grammar produces.

Who it's for

The intended user is a child or young learner — old enough to read a name and type it back, young enough that the joy of watching a character flip on command is still the central reward. No prior programming experience is assumed. The interface deliberately exposes a few primitives at a time rather than a full editor surface; the depth lives in composition, not in feature breadth.

The educational frame is two-fold: computational skill (naming, composition, sequencing, deterministic outcomes) and creative authorship (the artifacts you save are yours; nothing here is rated against a correct answer). Both lines of practice run through the same interface — every save adds to a personal library of named things that can be combined with everything else already saved.

Urban Griots Collaborative (UGC)

UGPlayground is built within the broader framework of Urban Griots Collaborative — an educational design practice focused on giving children rich, generative tools for self-expression and self-directed making, rooted in a griot-informed view of learning as composition, naming, and oral-tradition-style remix.

The principles UGC carries into this site:

  • Vocabulary as the primary unit of authorship. The alphabet is small and fixed; what learners build with it is open-ended. New vocabulary entries (a saved move, a saved shape) immediately become first-class composable pieces.
  • No correct answers, only legible behaviors. Every typed command either runs or doesn't; there's no quality judgment behind it. Failure is information about the grammar, not about the learner.
  • Composition surfaces emergence. Combining things that work alone reveals behaviors the learner didn't author and nobody scripted. That emergence is the reward structure.
  • Persistent personal libraries. The things you save are yours, named by you, and they persist across sessions. Returning to the site means returning to your own vocabulary, not starting over.

The site is one module within a larger educational design surface that UGC is developing; the labs you see here are early instances of a broader pattern (artifact → editor → library → invocation) intended to extend across other creative domains.

What you can do here

The current build ships three surfaces, all reachable from the playground:

  • Customize your avatar — pick an animal, a personality (which colors the rig), and a biome to live in.
  • Author moves in the Dance Lab — pose the character's limbs, attach a force vector, set rotations, name the move, and save it to your library.
  • Author shapes in the Shape Lab — compose polygons from a three-letter grammar, name them, and save them.
  • Invoke anything you saved — type a name, press space, and watch the character act it out (for moves) or watch the shape appear in the world (for stickers). Chain commands together with dots.

Full mechanical detail is in the Documentation.

Labs and learning outcomes

Each lab is a workspace for authoring artifacts — and a window onto one teachable idea. Two labs ship in this release; three more are sketched and planned.

Dance Lab Shipped
Naming a behavior so it can be re-invoked later — the shape of a function call before there's a word for it.
Shape Lab Shipped
How a small set of rules produces an open-ended language — the foundations of generative grammar.
Drum Lab Planned
Rhythm and rhythmic composition — pattern as a primitive that can be named, layered, and recalled.
Force Lab Planned
Authoring the forces of the world — gravity, push, pull — as rules the avatar moves through.
Game Lab Planned
What turns a sandbox into a game — win and loss states, authored rules, the boundaries of play.

Context

This release of UGPlayground was built as the term project for MET CS 601 — Frontend Web Application Development at Boston University Metropolitan College (Summer 1, 2026, Christian Hur). It is a single-author project and a single slice of a longer-running design practice.
